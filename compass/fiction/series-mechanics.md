# Series Mechanics

Complete reference for TV series writing: story engines, pilot structure, episode structure, A/B/C storylines, season arcs, and format specifications. Used by agents and commands when CLAUDE.md type = series.

---

## Story Engine

The mechanism that generates episodes. Without it, no series.

### Engine Types

| Type | How it generates episodes | Examples |
|------|--------------------------|----------|
| **Procedural** | External catalyst each episode (case, patient, crime) | House, CSI, Law & Order |
| **Serialized** | Continuous narrative — each episode is a chapter | Breaking Bad, Severance, Game of Thrones |
| **Hybrid** | Self-contained surface + serialized underneath | Mandalorian, Fargo, The Good Wife |
| **Relationship-driven** | Character dynamics generate situations | The Office, Succession, The Bear, Modern Family |
| **Mystery Box** | Central mystery revealed gradually | Severance, Lost, Westworld |
| **Anthology** | Shared format/theme, new cast each season | Black Mirror, White Lotus, True Detective |
| **Limited** | One continuous story in 6-10 parts | Chernobyl, Band of Brothers |

### Three Layers of Engine

| Layer | Scope | Example (Breaking Bad) |
|-------|-------|----------------------|
| **Episode engine** | One episode | A new cook, a new deal, a new threat |
| **Season engine** | One season | Walt builds an empire (S1: survival → S5: downfall) |
| **Series engine** | Entire run | Teacher → drug lord — each season a new moral floor |

Procedural survives on episode engine alone. Serialized needs season + series. Hybrid needs all three.

### The Irreconcilable Difference

The strongest engines are built on characters who want incompatible things and can never fully resolve that tension.

- Walt wants power. Jesse wants connection. (Breaking Bad)
- Carrie wants truth. Saul wants control. (Homeland)
- Each Roy child wants the company + father's love. Logan needs a successor but won't choose one. (Succession)

**If the core tension can be resolved in a single conversation — the engine is broken.**

### Central Dramatic Argument (Craig Mazin)

Every series argues a thesis demonstrated across every episode:
- "The system is designed to crush the individual" (The Wire)
- "Chemistry is the study of transformation" (Breaking Bad)
- "People don't change — they just become more of what they already are" (Mad Men)

### Engine Health Tests

1. **5-logline test**: generate five "the one where..." episode ideas in ten minutes. Can't → engine broken.
2. **10-episode pitch test**: can't pitch ten new episodes off your pilot → rethink engine.
3. **Pilot = mirror test**: pilot demonstrates the same engine as all subsequent episodes.
4. **Audience question test**: after each episode, viewer can articulate one question that makes them press "next."

### Engine Failure Modes

| Failure | Symptom | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| No plot movement | Characters return to starting positions | Late-run sitcoms becoming "hangout shows" |
| Flanderization | Traits cranked to extreme | The Simpsons (late seasons) |
| Engine without endpoint | Generates forever but has no destination | Lost (after S2) |
| Resolved tension | Core conflict solved too early | Shows that "jump the shark" after the couple gets together |
| Formula fatigue | Pattern predictable, no serialized layer | Late-run procedurals (NCIS S15+) |

## Character Engine

Where character decisions generate plot. The character's worldview creates cascading consequences.

### Film vs Series Characters

| Aspect | Film | Series |
|--------|------|--------|
| Arc | Full transformation | Comedy: often STATIC (engine needs them unchanged). Drama: transformation trajectory across seasons |
| Function | Drive one story | Generate infinite stories through decisions |
| Depth | Revealed over 2 hours | Revealed over seasons — layers peel back |

### Ensemble vs Single Protagonist

**Single protagonist**: viewer enters through one lens. Character changes radically. Simpler identification. (Breaking Bad, Barry, Fleabag)

**Ensemble — two models**:
1. **Friend Group**: central protagonist who can't complete journey without ensemble. (Harry Potter, Black-ish)
2. **Gang's All Here**: multiple stories with different arcs. Characters need each other for individual goals. (Game of Thrones, Modern Family)

### Character Web / Relationship Engine

Visual diagram of connections: family, friendship, rivalry, alliance, romance, conflict.
- Main character in center
- Lines to other characters with "what A thinks of B"
- When dynamics BETWEEN characters generate plot — that's the relationship engine

Succession: each child = separate relationship engine with father + between siblings. Configuration of alliances changes every season = new stories from same characters.

## Pilot Structure

### Pilot vs Feature Film

| Feature Film | TV Pilot |
|-------------|----------|
| Complete story | Pitch for infinite stories |
| Full character arc | Whiff of Change — hint of transformation |
| All questions answered | Open ends, hooks for return |
| Self-contained | Blueprint: producer must see how every future episode works |

### Three Laws of the Pilot (Save the Cat Writes for TV)

**1. Opening Pitch (first 2 minutes)**

Mini-movie working as standalone pitch for the entire show.

Examples:
- The Mandalorian: mini bounty hunt showcasing skills and lone-wolf nature
- Mrs. Maisel: wedding speech revealing personality, humor, voice
- Rick and Morty: complete mini-adventure showing the dynamic
- Breaking Bad: flash-forward in underwear with gun — stakes and tone instantly

**2. Guided Tour**

Show world rules through experience, not explanation. No "as you know, Bob" exposition.

**3. Whiff of Change**

NOT full arc — just a commitment, often spoken aloud:
- Mandalorian: from hunter to protector
- Barry: "I'm an actor"
- Breaking Bad: "I'm awake"

### Pilot Beat Sheet (Save the Cat adapted for TV)

Same 15 beats as film but with key difference: Final Image is a COMMITMENT, not a COMPLETION. The arc begins, not ends.

## Episode Structure

### A/B/C Storylines

**A-story**: main narrative per episode (engine-driven conflict)
**B-story**: parallel line echoing theme (often relationships)
**C-story/Runner**: recurring bit or season-long thread

| Format | A-story | B-story | C-story |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| 30-min comedy | ~9 beats | ~6 beats | 1-2 scenes max |
| 60-min drama | 8-10 beats | 5-6 beats | Small runner |
| Procedural | Case of the week | Personal life | Season arc |

Rules:
- A and B must thematically echo each other
- Cut between A and B at moments of tension, not resolution
- C-story appears 2-3 times maximum
- Every storyline has its own value shift

Scene distribution pattern (60-min): A-A-B-A-C-B-A-B-A-A

### Cold Open Types

| Type | When | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| Standalone gag | Comedy, tone-setter | Brooklyn Nine-Nine |
| Setup | Launches A-story | Breaking Bad |
| Flash-forward | Creates mystery | Better Call Saul |
| Tone marker | Pilot, genre shifts | Severance |

2-3 pages maximum. Must hook immediately.

### Act Breaks

| Platform | Acts | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Network 60-min | 5-6 acts | Cliffhanger before each commercial break |
| Network 30-min | 2-3 acts | Cold open + tag |
| Streaming | No rigid acts | Internal rhythm still matters |
| HBO-style | Feature-like | No visible act markers |

### Dan Harmon Story Circle (for 22-min comedy episodes)

| Step | Position | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| 1. You | 0-3 min | Character in comfort zone |
| 2. Need | 3-5 | Want something |
| 3. Go | 5-8 | Enter unfamiliar situation |
| 4. Search | 8-12 | Adapt, try solutions |
| 5. Find | 12-15 | Get what they wanted |
| 6. Take | 15-18 | Pay heavy price |
| 7. Return | 18-20 | Back to familiar |
| 8. Change | 20-22 | Changed (or pointedly unchanged in comedy) |

### 60-min Drama Episode Beats (streaming)

| # | Beat | Position | Function |
|---|------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Cold Open | 0-3 min | Hook or flash-forward |
| 2 | Setup | 3-10 | Episode question posed, B introduced |
| 3 | Complication | 10-20 | First obstacle, B develops |
| 4 | Escalation | 20-30 | Stakes rise, A/B intersect |
| 5 | Midpoint twist | ~30 | Reversal or new information |
| 6 | Consequences | 30-40 | Fallout, B crisis |
| 7 | Crisis | 40-48 | Worst case, B resolves |
| 8 | Climax | 48-55 | Question answered: YES-BUT or NO-AND |
| 9 | New status quo | 55-60 | Setup for next episode, cliffhanger |

## Season Arc

### Fractal Principle

Episode structure mirrors season structure mirrors series structure. Same dramatic shape at every scale.

| Point | Episode Position | Function |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| Pilot hook | Episode 1 | Opening Image of the season |
| Engine established | Episodes 1-2 | Set-Up: world, characters, engine running |
| First complication | Episodes 3-4 | Catalyst: equation changes |
| Midseason shift | Episodes 5-6 (of 10) | Midpoint: false victory/defeat, stakes escalate |
| Collapse | Episodes 7-8 | Bad Guys Close In + All Is Lost |
| Season climax | Episodes 9-10 | Finale: engine pushed to breaking point |
| Cliffhanger | Final scene | Sets up next season |

### Episode-to-Season Rules

Every episode must:
- Resolve its own A-story (give SOME closure, even in serialized shows)
- Advance at least one season-long thread
- End on YES-BUT or NO-AND (propel viewer to next episode)
- Plant or pay off at least one setup from previous episodes

### Serialized vs Procedural Balance

| Type | Episode closure | Season thread |
|------|----------------|---------------|
| Pure procedural | 100% resolved | Minimal |
| Hybrid | Case resolved, personal life continues | Strong |
| Serialized | Partial | Dominant |
| Full serial | Cliffhanger | Everything is season arc |

## Format Specifications

| Format | Pages | Acts | Notes |
|--------|-------|------|-------|
| 30-min comedy (network) | 22-25 | Cold open + 2 acts + tag | Fast pace, dialogue-heavy |
| 30-min comedy (streaming) | 25-35 | No rigid acts | Deeper characters, flexible length |
| 60-min drama (network) | 50-55 | Teaser + 4-5 acts | Cliffhanger each break |
| 60-min drama (streaming) | 45-65 | No act markers | Feels like mini-film |
| Limited series | 45-65/ep | 6-12 episodes total | Complete story, definite ending |

### Bottle Episodes

Minimal budget: limited locations, minimal cast, heavy dialogue. Used for:
- Budget savings (save money for expensive episodes)
- Deep character exploration
- Showcase writing craft

Examples: "Fly" (Breaking Bad), "The Chinese Restaurant" (Seinfeld)
