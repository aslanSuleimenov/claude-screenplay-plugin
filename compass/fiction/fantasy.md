## Definition

Living characters in invented worlds or situations that violate natural law. Magic, mythology, and magical creatures are real elements of the world — not hallucinations, not metaphors. The world has its own rules; violating them has consequences.

---

## Benchmarks

*The Lord of the Rings* trilogy (2001–03), *Pan's Labyrinth* (2006), *The Princess Bride* (1987), *Spirited Away* (2001), *Excalibur* (1981), *Princess Mononoke* (1997), *The Name of the Rose* (1986)

**Soviet:** *Kin-dza-dza!* (1986, Danelia) — absurdist sci-fi/fantasy; *The Scarlet Flower* (1952) — Soviet animated fairy tale; Ptushko fairy tale films (*Sadko*, *Ilya Muromets*)

---

## Structural conventions

1. **Established magic system / world rules** — internal logic must be consistent; magic can't solve everything
2. **Chosen One or outsider hero** — protagonist arrives from outside or discovers exceptional destiny
3. **Central battle of good vs. evil** — moral clarity is genre convention (even if characters are grey)
4. **Journey-initiation** — crossing into the magical world; trials; return changed
5. **Mentor who is lost** — guide who cannot complete the journey with the hero
6. **The cost of power** — magic or ability extracted through sacrifice

---

## Subtypes

- **High / Epic Fantasy** — secondary world, vast scope (Tolkien template)
- **Urban Fantasy** — magic hidden within contemporary urban setting
- **Dark Fantasy** — horror elements, morally ambiguous
- **Fairy Tale** — archetypal characters, folk narrative structure

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Sci-Fi:** fantasy uses magic; sci-fi uses extrapolated science/technology
- **vs. Horror:** fantasy's supernatural is wondrous or threatening-but-conquerable; horror's is existentially destabilizing
- **vs. Mythology:** mythology is sacred narrative; fantasy is secular world-building
