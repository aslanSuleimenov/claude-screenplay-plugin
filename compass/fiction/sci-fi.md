## Definition

Combines scientific or technological premise with speculative narrative. Explores social, political, and philosophical questions through the lens of the future or an alternative present. The "what if" as thought experiment.

---

## Benchmarks

*2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968, Kubrick), *Blade Runner* (1982), *Arrival* (2016), *The Matrix* (1999), *Solaris* (1972, Tarkovsky), *Stalker* (1979, Tarkovsky), *Metropolis* (1927), *Children of Men* (2006), *Her* (2013)

**Soviet (essential):** *Stalker* (1979, Tarkovsky) — philosophical sci-fi, Zone as metaphysical space; *Solaris* (1972, Tarkovsky) — consciousness and memory in space; *Kin-dza-dza!* (1986, Danelia) — absurdist Soviet sci-fi; *Dead Man's Letters* (*Письма мёртвого человека*, 1986, Lopushansky) — post-nuclear

## Structural conventions

1. **"What if" as premise** — one speculative change to reality, extrapolated rigorously
2. **Extrapolation of existing science/technology** — the speculative element must be internally consistent
3. **External change = social/moral metaphor** — the sci-fi premise encodes real-world anxiety
4. **Contact with the Radically Other** — aliens, AI, time-travelers, posthumans
5. **Protagonist as representative of "normal"** — the audience's anchor in a changed world
6. **The technology has a cost** — progress is not free; something human is lost

---

## Subtypes

- **Space Opera** — interstellar scale, adventure, civilization conflict
- **Cyberpunk** — high tech / low life; corporate dystopia; *Blade Runner*, *Ghost in the Shell*
- **Dystopia** — repressive perfected society as warning
- **Time Travel** — paradox, causality, regret
- **First Contact** — encounter with alien intelligence
- **Biopunk** — genetic manipulation, body modification

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Fantasy:** sci-fi uses extrapolated science; fantasy uses magic and myth
- **vs. Thriller:** sci-fi's stakes are civilizational; thriller's are personal
- **vs. Horror:** sci-fi's uncanny is cognitive; horror's is visceral
