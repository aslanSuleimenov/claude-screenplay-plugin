## Definition

Narrative plus musical numbers: characters transition from dialogue into song and dance. Two realities coexist — the narrative reality and the spectacular reality of performance. Music is not background; it is the character speaking their deepest truth.

---

## Benchmarks

*Singin' in the Rain* (1952), *The Wizard of Oz* (1939), *West Side Story* (1961), *La La Land* (2016), *All That Jazz* (1979), *Chicago* (2002), *Moulin Rouge!* (2001), *Cabaret* (1972)

**Soviet:** *Jolly Fellows* (*Весёлые ребята*, 1934, Alexandrov) — first Soviet sound musical; *Volga-Volga* (1938), *The Circus* (1936); *Carnival Night* (*Карнавальная ночь*, 1956, Ryazanov) — launching Gurchenko; *Hello, I'm Your Aunt!* (1975)

## Structural conventions

1. **Opening number sets the world** — establishes tone, rules of the musical universe
2. **"I Want Song"** — protagonist's desire stated through music in Act 1
3. **Love ballad as the inexpressible** — music says what dialogue cannot
4. **Reprise in altered emotional context** — same melody, opposite meaning
5. **Choreography as narrative information** — the dance tells us what the words hide
6. **Integrated vs. non-integrated** — integrated: songs emerge from story; non-integrated: performance numbers separate from plot

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Concert Film:** musical is narrative fiction with songs; concert film documents performance
- **vs. Opera:** musical uses spoken dialogue between numbers; opera is sung throughout
- **vs. Drama with soundtrack:** in a musical, characters sing *to each other* in the story world
