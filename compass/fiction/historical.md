## Definition

AFI defines Epic as "a genre of large scale with sweeping production scope." History as setting or theme. Individual fate as mirror of epoch. Scale — visual, temporal, moral — is a genre marker.

---

## Benchmarks

*Barry Lyndon* (1975, Kubrick), *Schindler's List* (1993), *Gandhi* (1982), *The Last Emperor* (1987), *Andrei Rublev* (1966, Tarkovsky), *Alexander Nevsky* (1938, Eisenstein), *Braveheart* (1995), *The Leopard* (1963, Visconti)

**Soviet (essential):** *Andrei Rublev* (1966, Tarkovsky) — medieval Russia, masterwork; *War and Peace* (*Война и мир*, 1966–67, Bondarchuk) — largest epic production in Soviet history; *Alexander Nevsky* (1938, Eisenstein) — propaganda as epic; *Ivan the Terrible* (1944–58, Eisenstein); *The Mirror* (*Зеркало*, 1975, Tarkovsky) — personal and historical memory

---

## Structural conventions

1. **Reconstruction of epoch through detail** — costume, speech, architecture as world-building
2. **Individual fate as mirror of era** — the personal story illuminates the historical moment
3. **Production scale as genre contract** — audiences expect visual grandeur
4. **Conflict between personal and historical** — private desire vs. historical obligation
5. **Catastrophe or transformation as mandatory element** — history has changed; the character has changed
6. **Time compression** — years or decades must be readable as dramatic units

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Biopic:** biopic centers on one consciousness; historical epic centers on events and their scale
- **vs. War film:** historical epic includes war but also courts, families, social transformations
- **vs. Adventure:** adventure seeks discovery; historical epic dramatizes known outcomes
