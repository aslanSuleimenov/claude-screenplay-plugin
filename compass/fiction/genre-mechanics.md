# Genre Mechanics

Structural contracts by genre. Each genre promises a specific emotional experience — the structure must deliver on that promise.

Commands read this file to validate scene function and flag missing structural obligations.

---

## How to use

For each genre: **Promise** = what the audience expects to feel. **Must happen** = structural events that cannot be skipped. **Forbidden** = what breaks the contract.

---

## Action

**Promise:** Physical escalation. Each sequence must be harder, higher-stakes, more costly than the last.

**Must happen:**
- Hero establishes competence early — then loses the advantage
- A point of no return where retreat becomes impossible
- The final confrontation physically costs the hero something real

**Forbidden:** Hero wins without sacrifice. Stakes plateau in Act II.

---

## Adventure

**Promise:** A journey that transforms the traveler. The destination matters less than who arrives.

**Must happen:**
- A call that is refused before it is accepted
- The hero loses what they brought with them (companion, tool, belief)
- The return — and the world at home looks different now

**Forbidden:** Hero returns unchanged. The journey is only external.

---

## Biography

**Promise:** The private logic behind a public life. Why this person, why this choice.

**Must happen:**
- A defining wound or belief established early
- A moment where the public persona and private person visibly split
- A reckoning — the cost of the life they chose

**Forbidden:** Chronological parade of achievements. No internal conflict.

---

## Black Comedy

**Promise:** The system is absurd and the joke is on everyone inside it.

**Must happen:**
- The protagonist attempts to fix something and makes it worse — repeatedly
- The logic of the world stays consistent (the absurdity has rules)
- The ending is funny and bleak simultaneously — no clean rescue

**Forbidden:** The protagonist escapes the absurdity unscathed. Sentimentality that resolves the darkness.

---

## Comedy

**Promise:** Chaos → order, but the order is better than what we started with.

**Must happen:**
- The protagonist's flaw drives the chaos
- A moment of genuine loss before the resolution (the comedy earns its ending)
- The resolution comes from the protagonist changing, not from luck

**Forbidden:** Stakes are never real. The protagonist is passive — chaos happens around them.

---

## Coming-of-Age

**Promise:** The last moment of a self that will not exist again.

**Must happen:**
- A belief about the world that the protagonist holds and cannot let go of
- An event that makes that belief impossible to maintain
- A choice — not forced — where the protagonist acts from their new self

**Forbidden:** The protagonist learns the lesson but doesn't pay for it. Adults solve the problem.

---

## Crime

**Promise:** The machinery of transgression — how it works, why it fails.

**Must happen:**
- The logic of the criminal world established and respected
- A point where escape was possible and the protagonist didn't take it
- Consequences proportional to the transgression

**Forbidden:** Crime succeeds without cost. The world's rules are inconsistent.

---

## Drama

**Promise:** A person under pressure reveals who they actually are.

**Must happen:**
- The protagonist wants something they cannot admit wanting
- External pressure forces an internal choice
- The ending shows what the pressure produced — change or the refusal to change

**Forbidden:** The protagonist's inner life is stated rather than shown. Resolution comes from outside.

---

## Fantasy

**Promise:** The rules of the other world illuminate the rules of this one.

**Must happen:**
- The rules of the fantastical world established and never violated
- The hero's real-world wound is the source of their power or weakness in the other world
- Return — and the real world is different because of what was learned

**Forbidden:** Magic solves the emotional problem. Rules are invented to serve plot.

---

## Heist

**Promise:** A perfect plan encountering imperfect reality.

**Must happen:**
- The plan laid out in detail — so the audience knows what can go wrong
- At least one element fails in a way that wasn't planned
- The crew dynamic fractures under pressure — trust is the real subject

**Forbidden:** The plan works exactly as described. The crew is interchangeable.

---

## Historical

**Promise:** The private human inside the public event.

**Must happen:**
- The historical event creates an impossible personal choice
- The protagonist acts against their own interest for something larger
- The cost of that choice is paid in full — history does not save them

**Forbidden:** History is backdrop. The protagonist is carried by events rather than choosing.

---

## Horror

**Promise:** The threat cannot be reasoned with. Safety is an illusion.

**Must happen:**
- The monster or threat established before the protagonist understands it
- Each attempt to stop the threat makes it worse or closer
- The "final" defeat costs something that cannot be recovered

**Forbidden:** The threat is explained away. The protagonist wins cleanly.

---

## Melodrama

**Promise:** Emotion at maximum pressure. Feelings that cannot be contained by ordinary behavior.

**Must happen:**
- A sacrifice that is genuine and costs the person making it
- The external obstacle must be truly insurmountable — not just inconvenient
- The emotional peak earned by accumulated pressure, not sudden revelation

**Forbidden:** Sentimentality substituted for earned emotion. Coincidence as resolution.

---

## Musical

**Promise:** Emotion so large it exceeds speech and becomes song.

**Must happen:**
- Each musical number advances the story or reveals inner state — it is not decoration
- The shift into music is motivated — the character has nowhere else to go
- The final number reflects how the protagonist has changed

**Forbidden:** Songs that pause the story. Numbers that repeat what dialogue already said.

---

## Mystery

**Promise:** Order has been broken and will be restored — but the restoration reveals something about the world that was always true.

**Must happen:**
- The crime or disappearance establishes the question — and plants the answer in plain sight
- The investigator's method reveals their character, not just their intelligence
- The solution reframes what the audience thought they understood

**Forbidden:** The solution depends on information withheld from the audience. The detective is infallible.

---

## Noir

**Promise:** The protagonist is already compromised. The question is how far they will fall.

**Must happen:**
- The protagonist makes a choice early that makes everything worse — and knows it
- The world is corrupt at the structural level — not one bad person but a system
- The ending is not rescue — it is reckoning

**Forbidden:** The protagonist is innocent. The ending restores order.

---

## Road Movie

**Promise:** Distance as mirror — the further from home, the more visible the self.

**Must happen:**
- A reason to leave that is not the real reason
- A traveling companion who represents what the protagonist refuses to see in themselves
- An encounter or event that makes going back impossible — or unnecessary

**Forbidden:** The journey is purely external. The destination is the point.

---

## Romantic Comedy

**Promise:** Two people who belong together fight it until they can't.

**Must happen:**
- An obstacle that is genuinely about character — not misunderstanding or coincidence
- A moment where the protagonist chooses something other than the relationship — and loses
- A grand gesture that is specific to these two people, not interchangeable

**Forbidden:** The obstacle is a misunderstanding that one conversation would solve. The ending is declared, not earned.

---

## Romantic Drama

**Promise:** Love that cannot survive the world — or survives by transforming both people.

**Must happen:**
- The relationship costs both people something real
- A moment where love and self-preservation are in direct conflict
- An ending that is honest about what love costs — not necessarily happy

**Forbidden:** Love conquers all without consequence. One character exists only to love the other.

---

## Sci-Drama

**Promise:** A technological or scientific premise that forces a human question the present cannot ask directly.

**Must happen:**
- The premise established and its rules never violated
- The speculative element creates an impossible personal choice — not just a plot problem
- The resolution is human, not technological

**Forbidden:** The technology is backdrop. The sci-fi element solves the emotional problem.

---

## Sci-Fi

**Promise:** The implications of one changed premise followed to their logical end.

**Must happen:**
- The changed premise established early and its rules respected throughout
- A point where the logic of the new world forces a choice that couldn't exist in the old one
- The ending reflects what the premise actually means — not a return to normal

**Forbidden:** Technological inconsistency. The premise is abandoned when inconvenient.

---

## Sport

**Promise:** The game as a mirror for the life outside it.

**Must happen:**
- The sport's rules and demands established so non-fans understand the stakes
- The protagonist's real obstacle is internal — the sport is the arena, not the subject
- The final competition is earned by what the protagonist has resolved or accepted

**Forbidden:** Winning the game resolves everything. The opponent is the only obstacle.

---

## Spy

**Promise:** Competence under impossible pressure — and the cost of living a divided life.

**Must happen:**
- The mission and the personal stakes established simultaneously — they must eventually collide
- A moment of genuine betrayal (by the protagonist or against them)
- The cost of loyalty — to country, to self, to the mission — paid in full

**Forbidden:** The spy is only competent. The divided life leaves no mark.

---

## Thriller

**Promise:** The protagonist cannot trust what they see. Every step forward is also a step into danger.

**Must happen:**
- Information withheld from the protagonist that the audience either shares or doesn't (both work — differently)
- Each attempt to reach safety closes one exit and opens another threat
- The revelation reframes what the protagonist — and audience — thought they understood

**Forbidden:** The threat is random. The protagonist is passive. The ending explains everything comfortably.

---

## War

**Promise:** What people become under conditions that strip away everything else.

**Must happen:**
- The group dynamic established — who each person is before the pressure begins
- A moment of moral choice under fire — not heroism, but the specific shape of this person's compromise
- The cost counted: not abstract, but named

**Forbidden:** War is only exciting. The cost is abstract. Characters are interchangeable.

---

## Western

**Promise:** A dying world and the last person who belongs to it.

**Must happen:**
- The code the protagonist lives by established — and then challenged by a world that has moved on
- A confrontation that cannot be avoided and cannot be won cleanly
- An ending that acknowledges what is being buried with the old world

**Forbidden:** The hero rides into a future. The violence has no weight.
