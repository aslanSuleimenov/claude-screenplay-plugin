## Definition

Genre defined by audience response — laughter. Light tone, resolution in harmony and happiness. Incongruity (something violates expectations) is the engine. Wide spectrum from slapstick to satire.

---

## Benchmarks

*Some Like It Hot* (1959), *The General* (1926, Keaton), *Groundhog Day* (1993), *Annie Hall* (1977), *The Grand Budapest Hotel* (2014), *This Is Spinal Tap* (1984), *Airplane!* (1980)

**Soviet:** *The Diamond Arm* (*Бриллиантовая рука*, 1969, Gaidai), *Ivan Vasilievich Changes His Profession* (1973, Gaidai), *The Caucasian Prisoner* (1967, Gaidai), *Office Romance* (*Служебный роман*, 1977, Ryazanov), *The Irony of Fate* (*Ирония судьбы*, 1975, Ryazanov) — foundational Soviet comedy canon

## Structural conventions

1. **Incongruity as engine** — character in wrong situation, misunderstanding, status reversal
2. **Protagonist's flawed reasoning** — they are wrong in a way that creates comic cascades
3. **Resolution restores social balance** — comedy ends with re-integration, not exclusion
4. **Comic timing** — pace and rhythm are craft elements as important as dialogue
5. **Stakes low enough to laugh** — or high stakes played as absurd
6. **Recognition humor** — audience laughs at what they recognize in themselves

---

## Subtypes

- **Slapstick / Physical** — body as comic instrument; Chaplin, Keaton, Tati
- **Screwball** — fast-talking, battle-of-sexes; 1930s Hollywood template
- **Satire** — targets social/political institutions through ridicule
- **Parody** — specific genre or text as comic target
- **Farce** — improbable situations, mistaken identity, extreme pace
- **Deadpan** — flat affect, absurd content; Jarmusch, Kaurismäki

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Dark Comedy:** comedy resolves in harmony; dark comedy uses tragedy as the joke
- **vs. Satire:** satire has a target and an argument; comedy's goal is laughter without agenda
- **vs. Romantic Comedy:** romcom requires the love story as structural requirement
