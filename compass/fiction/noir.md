# Film Noir / Neo-Noir

> Compiled from IMDB, Britannica, MasterClass, Wikipedia genre analysis.

## Definition

**Film Noir (classic):** Style and genre defined by cynical heroes, expressionist chiaroscuro lighting, and existential fatalism. Primarily post-WWII America. The world is corrupt; the hero knows it and is complicit.

**Neo-Noir:** Noir traits adapted to contemporary settings — color photography, explicit violence and sexuality, non-linear narrative, global settings.

---

## Benchmarks

**Classic Noir:** *Double Indemnity* (1944), *Sunset Boulevard* (1950), *The Maltese Falcon* (1941), *Out of the Past* (1947), *Touch of Evil* (1958), *Laura* (1944), *The Third Man* (1949)

**Neo-Noir:** *Chinatown* (1974), *Taxi Driver* (1976), *Blade Runner* (1982), *Blue Velvet* (1986), *No Country for Old Men* (2007), *Drive* (2011), *True Detective* (TV, 2014)

**Soviet/Russian:** *The Meeting Place Cannot Be Changed* (*Место встречи изменить нельзя*, 1979) — Soviet noir-detective hybrid; Balabanov's films carry noir fatalism throughout

## Structural conventions

1. **Femme fatale (classic)** — seduces and destroys; embodies the hero's weakness
2. **Unreliable narrator / flashbacks** — story told from the end, often in voiceover
3. **Hero violates moral law and pays** — compromise is the engine; redemption is impossible
4. **Venetian blind shadows, low-key lighting (classic)** — visual language as moral commentary
5. **Fatalism** — the protagonist knows they're trapped and goes forward anyway
6. **Investigation that reveals a corrupt world** — the crime is a surface; the rot goes deeper

---

## Neo-Noir additions

- Bright color palette (contrast with classic B&W)
- Female protagonist possible
- Non-linear structure (Pulp Fiction model)
- Genre self-awareness / quotation

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Crime/Gangster:** noir centers on the detective/investigator; gangster on the criminal
- **vs. Mystery:** mystery restores order through detection; noir reveals that order was never real
- **vs. Thriller:** thriller offers resolution; noir questions whether resolution is possible
