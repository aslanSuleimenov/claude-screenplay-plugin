## Definition

Romance + comedy as structural requirement. Both elements are obligatory: if the love story disappears, it's a comedy; if the humor disappears, it's a romantic drama. Meet-cute and final union are genre contracts.

---

## Benchmarks

*It Happened One Night* (1934), *When Harry Met Sally* (1989), *Four Weddings and a Funeral* (1994), *Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind* (2004), *His Girl Friday* (1940), *The Philadelphia Story* (1940), *Amélie* (2001)

**Soviet/Russian:** *Office Romance* (*Служебный роман*, 1977, Ryazanov) — Soviet romcom template; *The Irony of Fate* (*Ирония судьбы*, 1975) — New Year's Eve romcom; *Hello, I'm Your Aunt!* (1975, Korenev)

---

## Structural conventions

1. **Meet-cute** — amusing, memorable first encounter; establishes both characters in one scene
2. **Mutual attraction + obstacle** — they clearly belong together; something (usually themselves) prevents it
3. **Secondary characters as catalysts** — friends, family, exes accelerate or complicate
4. **Act 2 breakup** — the relationship collapses at the midpoint; all hope lost
5. **Grand romantic gesture** — protagonist earns the ending through public or sincere declaration
6. **Genre awareness** — modern romcoms often acknowledge the formula while honoring it

---

## Distinction from similar genres

- **vs. Romantic Drama:** romcom requires humor and happy ending; romantic drama needs neither
- **vs. Comedy:** romcom's central engine is the love story; comedy can be about anything
- **vs. Screwball Comedy:** screwball is a specific 1930s form; romcom is the modern evolution
