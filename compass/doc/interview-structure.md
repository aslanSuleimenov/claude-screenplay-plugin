# Interview as Dramatic Scene

Reference for documentary interview structure. An interview is not a delivery mechanism for information — it is a dramatic scene. It has a beginning, a turning point, and an end. Something must change by the time it's over.

---

## The interview as scene

Every interview has a **subject's inner conflict** — something they want to say and something they're resisting saying. The filmmaker's job is not to extract information but to create the conditions where that resistance becomes visible, or breaks.

A good interview ends somewhere different from where it started. The subject has revealed something — not necessarily a fact, but a position, a contradiction, a emotion they didn't plan on. If the subject says exactly what they came prepared to say, the interview has failed as a scene.

**Three structural moments to look for:**

1. **Entry** — who does the subject present themselves as? What is their controlled version of the story?
2. **Fracture** — the moment where the controlled version cracks. A pause, a reframe, an unexpected answer. This is the scene's turning point.
3. **Exit** — where do they land? Deeper in their position, or somewhere they didn't expect?

If the interview has no fracture — it's testimony, not drama. Testimony has its place (establishing facts, context), but it shouldn't be confused with a dramatic scene.

---

## Point of view is not bias

A documentary is always an argument. It has a filmmaker's point of view — a position on the material. This is not bias; it's the film's spine.

A film without a point of view is not neutral — it's empty. The audience can feel the absence. They don't know what to do with the material because no one has told them what it means.

**The filmmaker's POV operates on three levels:**

1. **Selection** — which subjects, which moments, which footage was chosen and which was left out
2. **Juxtaposition** — what comes before and after each interview or sequence
3. **Omission** — what the film deliberately doesn't show or explain

The POV doesn't have to be stated. It should be felt in structure and choice. Voice-over that announces the thesis is usually a sign the images haven't done their job.

---

## Interview placement in structure

Interviews are not interchangeable. Each interview appears at a specific structural moment and must do a specific job:

| Placement | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Opening interview | Establish the central question — not answer it |
| Act I interviews | Build the world, introduce contradictions |
| Midpoint interview | Complicate the picture — the version that seemed true gets challenged |
| Crisis interview | The subject under maximum pressure — fracture most likely here |
| Closing interview | Not summary. The subject after something has changed — or refused to change |

---

## Red flags in interview-driven documentaries

- Every interview says the same thing in different words — no contradiction, no complexity
- Interviews are used only to deliver facts that could be conveyed by other means
- The filmmaker never appears in the structure — no sense of who is asking and why
- The closing interview restates the opening position — no journey

---

## For documentary AV-script: checking interview blocks

When writing or reviewing a `# Block` that contains SOT (Sound on Tape / interview):

- What is the subject's position at the start of this block?
- Does anything in this block challenge or develop that position?
- What does the viewer know after this block that they didn't before — not as fact, but as understanding?
- Is the SOT chosen for what it reveals, or just for what it says?
